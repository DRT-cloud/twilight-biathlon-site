WHAT IS RUN AND GUN?

A Beginner's Guide

Run and gun (also called "run n' gun" or "tactical biathlon") is a timed shooting competition that combines cross-country running with live-fire shooting stages. Competitors carry their firearms throughout the entire course, stopping at designated stages to engage targets with rifles, pistols, or pistol-caliber carbines, then continue running to the next stage.

How It Works

A typical run-and-gun event works like this:

  1. Start. You begin at the starting line with all your gear: firearms, ammunition, eye/ear protection, and lighting (for night events). You carry everything for the entire course.
  2. Run. Follow a marked course through terrain — fields, woods, hills, trails. Distances range from 2K to 10+ miles depending on the event.
  3. Shoot. At each shooting stage, a Range Officer (RO) supervises you through a course of fire. Engage targets with your designated firearm(s), then move on.
  4. Repeat. Run to the next stage. Shoot. Run. Until you finish the course.
  5. Score. Your final score is your total time: running time + shooting stage times + any penalty for missed targets. Lowest total time wins.

What Makes It Different

Run and gun combines two skills that most shooting sports treat separately: physical fitness and marksmanship under stress. After running uphill through a field, your heart rate is elevated, your hands may be shaking, and you need to settle onto a target at 100+ yards. That combination is the appeal.

Unlike static range competitions (USPSA, IDPA, PRS), run and gun rewards athletes who can both run and shoot. You can’t win on marksmanship alone if your run time is slow, and you can’t win on fitness alone if you’re stacking penalties at every stage.

What Twilight Biathlon Adds

Twilight Biathlon takes the run-and-gun format and runs it at night — on completely unlit terrain. That adds three layers of difficulty:

  • Navigation. The course is marked, but you’re running through Oklahoma fields, woods, hills, and creek beds in the dark. Your light discipline and route-finding matter.
  • Target identification. You must positively identify every target with your own lighting or night vision before engaging. No firing at shadows.
  • Equipment management. Lights, batteries, NV devices, IR illuminators — managing your equipment while moving through darkness is an additional skill layer.

Four divisions span two nights. Friday is NV-only (NV 2Gun and NV PCC). Saturday is white-light divisions (2Gun and PCC) where headlamps and weapon lights are permitted. This is a unique event that dedicates an entire night exclusively to night vision competitors.

Do I Need to Be Fast?

No. The run-and-gun community is one of the most welcoming in competitive shooting. Many competitors walk significant portions of the course. Finishing is the goal.

If you’ve never done a run and gun before, the Twilight Biathlon’s 5K course is on the shorter end of the format (many events run 5–10+ miles). It’s a good entry point.
